Welcome to Dr. Sandro Tsang’s Homepage

I have been awarded a PhD in Empirical Economics, and have worked in academic institutes of several countries. I have been a tutor on a master’s degree programme accredited by the EUCLID University since 2012. I also work with NextGenU.org to revise public health courses delivered to graduate students of medical schools. The duties drive me to learn evidence-based medicine research methodologies applicable to clinical practice and health policy-making.

Clinical reasoning is my primary research interest. I consider understanding clinical behaviours critical for deriving operationalisable healthcare policies, but this topic is scarcely covered in economics textbooks. I derived a Mathematical model that captures the uncertainties involved in prescribing antibiotics and the consequences as a 3-month MSc dissertation (here is a published version). I subsequently researched into other clinical decision problems, including eHealth, influenza-like illnesses, etc. My PhD thesis explored impacts of implementing eHealth to facilitate clinical reasoning from the perspectives of epistemology and practising humanistic medicine. I was keen on further developing this eHealth model. My cold-call skill let me luckily reach Professor Michael Loughlin (I was not aware of his prominence in the philosophy of humanistic medicine and its related research). My ideas intrigued him to guide me to further the research. Without his guidance, I might not be motivated to write up an interdisciplinary theory that awarded me an ESPCH Essay Prize (reputed researchers were also in the competitor pool). This theory portrays Arrow Physician to be an ideal medical practitioner in modern health systems. S/he is required to deliver patient-centred care in a humanistic approach, and to reason like an economist at the same time. I postulate that establishing polycentric health systems is crucial to substantiate her/his mission, and to improve healthcare resource allocations through collective ethical actions. My research also overlaps with policy-making for infectious disease surveillance and mitigating antimicrobial resistance. It also involves turning my self-taught skills in programming (e.g., R, python, SQL, bash, etc.) into actions.

I mostly apply programming skills to analytic or typesetting levels. However, I have spotted room for improving R packages koRpus, mice and miceadds. I do practise the art of programming – solving problems efficiently with basic and minimal commands. This practice is shown in the codes stored in my GitHub repos. I only present some R and python codes there. A techy will know that this webpage demonstrates applications of four computational languages.

You may know more about me from LinkedIn or twitter. I may share various academic topics and some computation tricks here. I welcome your approaching me through email or filling in the form. You have been directed to my GitHub repos. The X-ray-Pytools include tools for quality check, editing,... building convolutional neural networks from X-ray images. I was a novice of computer vision and developed the full-stack skills for handing image data for a 3-month project.

The scrapping scripts were written to serve my curisoity. I find that scrapping Google Scholar (GS) is increasingly applied to conduct systematic literature review. The researchers either scrapped the first 100 abstracts or they never explained how they scrapped all the abstracts.

The first approach does not fulfill an exhaustive search criterion of systematic review. We know that the robot.txt of GS prohibits us from scrapping more than 100 abstracts. The second approach can be implemented by scrapping the downloaded webpages. I created a timer to download the webpages in random time intervals. I have never implemented this approach in my own research, because I am not wealthy enough to enter into a legal battle with the conglomerate. I wrote up the scripts to simply verify the claim. They may be excuted for other journal databases. Scrapping GS violates its user policies.
