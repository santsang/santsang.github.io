Welcome to Dr. Sandro Tsang’s homepage

I have been awarded a PhD in Empirical Economics, and working in academic institutes of several countries. I have been a tutor on the Public Health master’s programme in the Peoples’ Open Access Education Initiative since 2012. I also work with NextGenU.org to revise public health courses delivered to graduates of medical schools. The duties drive me to learn evidence-based medicine research methodologies applicable to clinical practice and policy-making.

Clinical reasoning is my primary research interest. I consider understanding clinical behaviours critical for deriving operationalisable healthcare policies, but this topic is scarcely covered in economics textbooks. I derived a Mathematical model that capture the uncertainties involved in prescribing antibiotics and the consequences as a 3-month MSc dissertation (here a result of further research). I subsequently researched into other clinical decision problems, including eHealth, influenza-like illnesses, etc. My PhD thesis explored physicians’ perceived impacts of implementing eHealth on their practice from the perspectives of epistemology and practising humanistic medicine. I was keen on further developing this eHealth model. My cold-call skill let me luckily reach Professor Michael Loughlin (I was not aware of his prominence in the philosophy of humanistic medicine and its related research). My ideas intrigued him to guide me to further the research. Without his guidance, I might not be motivated to write up an interdisciplinary theory that awarded me an ESPCH Essay Prize (reputed researchers were also in the competitor pool). This theory portrays Arrow Physician to be an ideal medical practitioner in modern health systems. S/he is required to deliver patient-centred care in a humanistic approach, and to reason like an economist at the same time. I postulate that establishing polycentric health systems is crucial to substantiate her/his mission, and to improve healthcare resource allocations through collective ethical actions. My research also overlaps with policy-making for infectious disease surveillance and mitigating antimicrobial resistance. It also involves turning my self-taught programming skills (e.g., R, python, SQL, bash, etc.), machine learning and deep learning into actions.

I only apply programming skills to analytic levels. However, I have spotted room for improving R packages koRpus, mice and miceadds. I do practise the art of programming – solving problems with basic and minimal commands. It is shown in the source codes of this webpage, and some snippets of R and python available from GitHub repos and Gist.
